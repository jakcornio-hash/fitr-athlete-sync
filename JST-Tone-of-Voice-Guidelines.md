## THE CORE PRINCIPLE

The goal isn't just clean copy. It's copy that sounds like a real person who's actually been in the room.

Four shifts matter most:

1. **Lead with specific moments, not declarations.** AI writes "the truth is most athletes plateau because..." A human writes "I spoke to an athlete last week who'd hit the same snatch weight for six months."
2. **Include yourself in the pattern.** "I've done this too. Still catch myself doing it." Pulls the reader in instead of lecturing them.
3. **Specific beats polished every single time.** "One of our athletes put 8kg on her snatch in 12 weeks" is a million times more powerful than "our athletes see massive progress." The more specific, the more real.
4. **If in doubt, go more simple.** If an athlete has to read it twice to understand what to do, it needs rewriting. This applies to everything — captions, coaching notes, daily primers, WhatsApp messages. Plain language always wins.

If a line could be written by someone who's never coached an athlete in their life, it needs rewriting.

**The read-it-out-loud test:** If you'd feel like a knobhead saying it to someone's face at the gym, rewrite it. If it sounds weird when you say it, it's weird when they read it.

**Never:** "Alright," "Yo," "Mate," as standalone openers. "Hi there." "Hi [Name]!" with exclamation. "So glad..." Anything with an emoji.

## SIGN-OFFS

**Email:** `Cheers, Jak` or `Cheers, Jak Cornthwaite`
**WhatsApp / DM / Slack:** Just `Jak` on its own line.
**Never:** "Kind regards." "Best wishes." "Speak soon." "– Jak" (with dash). "Let's go!" with rocket emoji.

**Automated sends (no sign-off):** Anything the system sends on its own, the weekly
email, the month in review, the automated Fitr messages, carries no sign-off at all.
Those aren't personally from Jak and shouldn't pretend to be.

**Drafted messages (the coach who sends it signs it):** A draft a coach reads and
sends is from that coach, so it goes out with their name. Ed's messages sign `Ed`,
Denis's sign `Denis`. Never sign a coach's name to something they didn't send, and
never default to `Jak` because he's the head coach. If nobody has said who's sending
it, leave it off rather than guess.

## LANGUAGE RULES

### Always

- **Contractions throughout** — you're, we're, don't, can't, it's, you've got, could've, would've. No exceptions.
- **UK spellings** — programme, practising, specialised, dialled, prioritising, optimise, behaviour.
- **Explain the "why" before the ask.**
- **End with one clear next action.**
- **Plain language over jargon.** If a term needs explaining, either explain it immediately or replace it.
- **Active voice, not passive.** "Are you struggling to close leads?" not "Do leads feel harder to close?" Say "you" not "it." Direct, not floaty.
- **Keep analogies short.** One or two sentences max. If it needs a whole paragraph to explain the metaphor, just explain the actual thing instead.
- **No em dashes (—) in the copy.** Overuse of the em dash is one of the clearest AI tells. Never use them inside sentences. Replace with a full stop (start a new sentence), a comma (if it's a small aside), or rewrite the sentence so it doesn't need one. "A sandbag fights back — the load shifts mid-carry" becomes "A sandbag fights back. The load shifts mid-carry." This applies to every piece of copy: captions, emails, DMs, WhatsApp, slides, briefs. Hyphens in compound words (world-class, 1-on-1, 16-17) are fine — it's only the sentence-connecting dash that's banned.

### Words & phrases that fit

mate, wicked, belting, epic, graft, graft smarter (captions only), double down, sticky, dialled in, crack on, get it sorted, get it locked in, get stuck in, stop guessing, world-class basics, inches (marginal gains), LMK, bits and bobs, buzzing, fair enough, to be fair, that sort of stuff, no worries, hope you're good, let me know, dead legs (not "degraded legs"), food (not "nutrition" in athlete-facing copy)

### Never

- "Brutal" or "painful" as self-descriptors of own programming
- "Boring" as a self-descriptor of programming
- "Clinical" as a tone word
- "Junk volume," "CNS," "muscular occlusion," "alactic," "eccentric cardiac hypertrophy," "Stretch-Shortening Cycle" — without plain explanation
- The hardware/software/hardwire family — "fix your software," "hardwire the movement," "rebuild the hardware," "software leaked," "software upgrade"
- Car metaphors (chassis, torque, suspension, engine as extended metaphor) — 16-Year-Old Rule. Short references to "engine" for conditioning are fine. Extended car analogies aren't.
- American slang — "shooters," "for $0," "wacked," "lone wolf" military language
- Corporate jargon — synergize, circle back, leverage, operational nightmare, optimize, streamline, done-for-you, simultaneously (use "at the same time")
- "Nutrition" in athlete-facing copy — use "food" instead
- "Degraded" — use "dead" (e.g. "dead legs" not "degraded legs")
- "Microcosm," "rehearsed" (in coaching context), "audit" (in customer-facing copy — fine internally)
- "Assessment" for calls — use "call" or "chat" instead
- "I'll automatically send you..." (breaks coach-to-athlete tone)
- Emojis in professional emails, designed graphics, paid ads, or Instagram captions
- Numbered emojis (1️⃣ 2️⃣ 3️⃣) in any content
- Section header emojis (🥷💀🧪🪨🎯🤸🚨⭕🏋️🔥🧠🦍🚀) — bold text headers do the same job
- Hashtags on captions — don't fit the voice
- Swearing in designed graphics or paid ad copy (face-to-camera reels only)
- "So glad..." or over-enthusiastic openers in DMs

### Internal framework names — NEVER in customer-facing copy

These are for internal team briefs only. Strip them from any external copy:

Business-in-a-Box, The Ledger, Full Gü, The Chimp, Red Head / Blue Head, Athlete IQ, W.I.N. (What's Important Now), tactical breaking, The Recon, The Arena, The DNA Audit, Bottleneck Call, Quantum Leap, Ethical Bribe, Tollbooth, A-C-A Framework, Sniper Audit, Accumulators, Separators, MBR, 40% Rule, TUT Tax, Orange Zone, Red Switch, Banker, Minting, Hail Mary, Levels 1/2/3, JST Army, Zero-Slop, Golden Cue, Guided Discovery, co-regulation (as a label), adaptation currency

Also strip from customer copy: any "branded" labels for coaching concepts (e.g. "The Post-Failure Diagnostic," "The Ego Check," "The Clinical Standard," "The System Reveal"). Just describe what actually happened.

Internal frameworks are fine in team briefs. They're a red flag in anything a customer will read.

## THE 16-YEAR-OLD RULE

If a 16-year-old athlete couldn't follow the sentence, it needs rewriting. Never stack two technical terms in one sentence. If you use a term that needs explaining, explain it immediately or replace it.

**Instead of:** "We target alactic capacity to push your anaerobic threshold."
**Use:** "We work the short, hard efforts that teach your body to recover faster between sets."

**Instead of:** "Your central nervous system fatigues before your muscles do."
**Use:** "The stuff that needs the most skill and power has to go first. Once your body's fatigued, your technique breaks down."

**Instead of:** "Aerobic training suppresses your force output for hours."
**Use:** "Get the order or the spacing wrong and you're lifting on dead legs."

This rule applies even harder for Junior Youth Squad content where parents are reading it too.

## THE AI TELLS — WHAT TO AVOID

These are the dead giveaways that content was written by AI. Cut them on sight. Split into three categories: banned phrases, structural habits, and hollow hype.

### 10 banned phrases

These sound like nothing a real person would ever say. If any show up in copy, replace them immediately.

1. **"Delve into" / "Unpack"** — Nobody says delve. Use "look at" or "break down."
2. **"This underscores the importance of"** — AI connecting two ideas with a fancy bridge. Just say the thing.
3. **"In today's fast-paced world"** — Says nothing. Means nothing. Delete it and start with the actual point.
4. **"Holistic approach"** — What does that actually mean? Tell people specifically what you do instead.
5. **"Embark on your journey"** — Has anyone ever said embark in a text message? No. Cut it.
6. **"Unlock your potential"** — Your audience has seen this on 40 other coach profiles this week. It lands on nobody.
7. **"Tailored to your unique needs"** — Every coach says this. Stop claiming it and show it instead.
8. **"Moreover" / "Furthermore" / "Additionally"** — GCSE essay words. Nobody uses them when they're actually talking to another person. Just write a new sentence.
9. **"It's important to note that"** — AI hedging. If it's important, just say it.
10. **"Empower" used as filler** — Not terrible on its own, but AI uses it to fill space instead of telling people what you actually do for them.

**The test for all of these:** If you wouldn't say it stood in front of someone at the gym, get rid of it.

### 5 structural habits that give AI away

You could swap every dodgy phrase out and it'd still read like AI wrote it if the structure gives it away.

1. **The "It's not X, it's Y" reframe** — "It's not about motivation, it's about discipline." AI trying to sound punchy. Comes across as try-hard. Most people clock it now.
2. **"The truth:" followed by a colon** — "The truth: most coaches are leaving money on the table." See also "the brutal truth," "the harsh reality," "the result." One or two words teeing up the actual point, followed by a colon. Everywhere on fitness Instagram. Sounds smart. Reads as lazy.
3. **Forced negatives** — "Content that converts, not confuses." "Results that last, not fade." "Less about X. More about Y." Sticking a negative after a positive in a way nobody would ever say out loud. Sticks out a mile.
4. **Overly formal words that sound weird in context** — "lean in," "navigate," "transformation," "emerging," "-centric," "simultaneously," "degraded." Normal words on their own, but if a word sounds forced or odd in the sentence, it's probably AI.
5. **Passive voice** — AI shifts things into the passive and makes everything feel floaty and removed. Real people talk directly. They say "you" not "it."

### Hollow hype words

Cut on sight: unlock, elevate, transform, revolutionise, game-changer, next-level, dominate (unless face-to-camera), crush it, empower (as filler), navigate (in non-literal use), lean in, -centric, world-class (except "world-class basics" which is Jak's phrase), set the absolute standard, blew this out of the water, set the bar incredibly high

### Declaration openers

Cut on sight: "The truth is...," "The brutal truth about...," "Here's what nobody's telling you...," "Stop [X]. Start [Y].", "The numbers speak for themselves.", "The data is clear."

### Generic authority flexes

Cut on sight: "Most people self-sabotage...," "9 out of 10 athletes...," "What separates the best from the rest..."

## HOW TO SOUND HUMAN INSTEAD

### 1. Lead with a specific moment or person
AI: *"Most athletes plateau because they ignore the basics."*
Human: *"Spoke to an athlete last week who's been stuck on the same snatch for six months. First thing we looked at wasn't strength — it was her setup position."*

### 2. Include yourself in the pattern
AI: *"You need to stop skipping warm-ups."*
Human: *"I used to skip my warm-up every session. Still catch myself doing it on busy days. Here's what changed."*

### 3. Use specific numbers, names, timeframes
AI: *"Our athletes see massive progress."*
Human: *"One of our athletes put 8kg on her snatch in 12 weeks. She wasn't training more — she was training different."*

AI: *"I help busy people reach their health goals."*
Human: *"I helped a 42-year-old dad working night shifts drop 16kg in 5 months."*

### 4. Observation over declaration
AI: *"The truth is, intent matters more than volume."*
Human: *"I keep seeing the same thing with new athletes in their first month..."*

### 5. Swap "the brutal truth about..." for what you actually see
AI: *"The brutal truth about why you're not getting stronger."*
Human: *"I see this with almost every athlete in the first few weeks. They're grafting — but doing one thing that's cancelling the rest out."*

### 6. Show what you do, don't dress it up
AI: *"We take a holistic approach to your fitness journey."*
Human: *"We look at your training, your food, and your sleep — because all three affect your results."*

### 7. Replace the hedge with an actual number
AI: *"It's important to note that results may vary."*
Human: *"Results vary. But here's what most people see in the first 8 weeks."*

### 8. Kill the fancy bridge, just start
AI: *"This underscores the importance of consistency in your training."*
Human: *"Consistency isn't optional. It's the whole thing."*

### 9. Let the numbers do the talking
AI: *"Georgia Ayres set the absolute standard with an incredible 11:00 finish."*
Human: *"Georgia Ayres took it at 11:00."*

When athletes hit impressive numbers, you don't need to tell people it's impressive. Just say the number and let it land.

## AUDIENCE-SPECIFIC TONE

### Athletes (WhatsApp community group)
- Open: `Morning team,` or `Evening team,`
- Plain language, encouraging but honest
- No emojis
- Sign off: `Jak` (own line)
- Purpose: keep athletes informed, motivated, and bought in to the programme

### Parents (Junior Youth Squad)
- Softer sell than athlete content
- No jargon at all — the 16-Year-Old Rule applies even harder because parents are reading it too
- Don't assume parents understand CrossFit terminology
- CTA is always NEXTGEN, never DIAGNOSTIC
- The goal is trust and reassurance, not hype

### Gym owners (JST Class)
- Peer-to-peer tone — they're coaches, not corporate clients
- Business language is fine but still plain
- Treat them like mates who run gyms
- CTA is always AFFILIATE

### Team / coaches (internal Slack / WhatsApp)
- Direct, blunt, honest
- Internal framework names are fine here
- Feedback can be straight ("that is really confusing to read," "this needs to be simpler")
- Sign off: just `Jak` or nothing

### Bespoke enquiries (DMs)
- Open: `Hey [First Name],`
- Warm but not salesy — never over-enthusiastic ("So glad!", "Amazing!")
- Always offer a call as the next step
- Keep it short — three to four lines max
- Don't say "no sales pitch" or "no obligation" — it draws attention to the fact it could be one. "Just a conversation" is cleaner.
- Sign off: `Jak` (own line)

### Coaching notes / daily primers (in-app on Fitr)
- Must be readable by an athlete on their phone at the gym
- If they have to read it twice to understand what to do, rewrite it
- No jargon, no coaching textbook language
- Keep it short — one to two sentences per note
- If in doubt, go more simple

## FORMAT PATTERNS BY CHANNEL

### Email
- Subject: plain, conversational, no caps, no emojis
- Open: `Hey [First Name],`
- Short paragraphs. Contractions throughout. One clear CTA.
- Close: `Cheers, Jak`

### WhatsApp (team / community)
- Open: `Morning team,` or `Evening team,`
- Context first, then the ask.
- Plain prose. No numbered emoji lists. No section header emojis.
- Link if needed.
- Sign off: `Jak` (own line)

### Instagram captions
- Flowing prose with contractions.
- Lead with a specific moment or observation, not a declaration.
- One CTA line at the end.
- No numbered emojis. No hashtags. No section header emojis.
- No "It's not X, it's Y" reframes. No "The truth:" setups. No forced negatives.
- Bold section labels are fine for scannable captions (e.g. base-building guidelines) — they do the job emojis were doing without looking like a newsletter.

### Instagram carousels
- Hook slide needs to stop the scroll — counterintuitive statements or specific claims work best
- One idea per slide. Short sentences.
- Bold all-caps statements work for philosophy carousels
- Coaching primers in italics or a highlight colour on programming carousels
- CTA on the final slide — one line, one keyword
- No branded framework names on any slide

### Reel scripts
- Face-to-camera, conversational
- Swearing is fine in Jak's natural delivery
- Looser than written copy — write how you'd actually say it
- Keep technical explanations to plain language (16-Year-Old Rule still applies)
- CTA at the end should feel like a conversation, not a sales pitch

### ManyChat / DMs
- Opening DM: short. Handshake only. No pitch.
- Link DM: frame the value. One CTA.
- Follow-up DM (2–4 hrs later): low pressure. Door open.
- Never: "Welcome to the JST Army" or any branded internal language in cold DMs.
- No emojis.

### Paid ads (designed / static copy)
- No swearing.
- No emojis in the graphic.
- Clean copy. No branded framework names.

### Block wrap-ups / leaderboard posts
- Named athletes with specific numbers — that's the strength of these posts
- Break into short sections with bold headers (not emoji headers)
- Let the numbers do the talking — don't add hype on top ("set the absolute standard," "blew this out of the water")
- Include consistency shoutouts — athletes who showed up across the full block
- End with a teaser for what's next in the programme

## WHEN IN DOUBT

If you're about to write content and don't have a real example to pull from, **ask Jak for one.** A real client, a real conversation, a real number — always beats a generalisation. Better to pause and ask than write something that sounds like every other coach on the internet.

**If in doubt, go more simple.** That's the rule. If a sentence reads like a coaching textbook, simplify it. If a coaching note could confuse an athlete reading it on their phone between sets, rewrite it. Plain language always wins.

## QUICK CHECK BEFORE SENDING ANYTHING

1. Does it open with a declaration or a moment? *(Moment = good.)*
2. Any of the 10 banned phrases in there? *(Delve, unpack, holistic, embark, unlock, empower, moreover, furthermore, "important to note," "fast-paced world")*
3. Any of the 5 structural tells? *("It's not X it's Y," "The truth:," forced negatives, passive voice, overly formal words)*
4. Any hardware/software language? *(Hardwire, software, rebuild the hardware — strip it.)*
5. Does every paragraph start with a transition word? *(If yes — rewrite the openings.)*
6. Are the contractions in? *(you're, don't, we've got, could've)*
7. UK spellings? *(programme, not program)*
8. Active voice throughout? *(Say "you" not "it." Direct, not floaty.)*
9. Any internal framework names leaking into customer copy? *(Strip them.)*
10. Is the right CTA keyword being used for this audience? *(Check the CTA list above.)*
11. Does it end with "Let's get to work" or "Ready to take it to the next level"? *(If yes — rewrite.)*
12. One clear CTA, not three?
13. Sign-off right for the channel? *(Cheers, Jak / just Jak)*
14. Is there a sentence you can't explain in plain English? *(Get rid of it.)*
15. Does the ending work for literally any coach anywhere, or is it actually specific to this post?
16. Any emojis that shouldn't be there? *(No emojis in captions, emails, WhatsApp, designed graphics, or ads.)*
17. Are the numbers doing the talking, or is there hype stacked on top? *(Let the numbers land.)*
18. Any em dashes (—) in the copy? *(Replace with full stops, commas, or a rewrite. Compound-word hyphens are fine.)*
19. **Would you actually say this out loud to someone?** *(If not, rewrite it.)*

If all nineteen pass, it's ready.
